Wildfires in the United States

Visualizing 24 years of wildfire data

Remember the last time you had a bonfire?


Being around family and friends while roasting marshmellows in the cold weather sounds like a pleasant holiday night for most of us. Fires such as these fall under the controlled fires category. Contolled fires are good - they provide warmth, light, and means for cooking food among other things. But what happens when things start to become uncontrolled?

A recent report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change has determined that dramatic and rapid changes to the global economy are required in order to avoid increasing climate-related risks for natural and human systems.

Through our visualizations, we aim to tell the truth about the status quo with regard to wildfires in the United States of America. First, we present aggregated national data through many separate visualizations, each with lightweight interactions, to give the user an overview of the data. Then, we present a visualization with many interactions to enable the user to drill down and explore the data by state. Finally, we guide the user to resources they could use to make a difference.

How has amount of land burned in a year changed?

Use this tool to explore how the total number of acres burned changed over years. You can explore the total area burned in both state level (left) and national level (right).
Play the animation to see the changes over years. Click the Next or Previous button to the years you want to see. Hover over the map or bar to get the number of total acres burned.
Darker color represents a larger area burned.

The total area burned of US fluctuates from year to year, but the overall trend is upward. The data seems to fluctuate periodically, with widespread fires occurring every year or two.
From 1992 to 2015, fires were mainly concentrated in the West and South, and the fire area was also relatively large. Such as Texas, California, Alaska, and Idaho. The western and southern states account for a very large share of the total area burned in the United States.

How has the degree of fires distributed among years?
This visualization helps to understand the distribution of fires at different classes and the number of fires at different classes. The fire level is divided into 7 levels, from A to G, representing the area burned from small to large. The linear distribution cannot perfectly show the distribution of fire size classes and the number of fires at each class, since the area burned of class A is extremely small and the area burned of class G is extremely large. Therefore, we decided to use log (base 10) to narrow the gap between A and G, so that we could better see the distribution of fires at different levels and how many they occurred.

We used the whole data from 1992 to 2015 to plot this scatterplot. Each point in the plot represents a fire event, with different colors representing its class. The horizontal axis is the date it occurred, and the vertical axis is its acres burned. We can clearly see that as fires grow in size, the occurrence of fires become less frequent (extremely dense at class A and B, more sparse at class F and G). Therefore, lower fire size classes tend to happen more often and higher fire size classes tend to happen less often.

What are the causes of the most destructive fires?

Only the top 1% of most destuctive fires (defined by the number of acres burned) for a given year are shown in this plot. Fire causes have been sorted by number of fires represented in this plot.

While lightning strikes are a prevalent cause for fires, many of the most destructive fires have man-made origins. These include fires triggered by arson, equipment use, and campfires.
The number of fires across all the states in the US for each cause is shown in this plot. Hover over the legend to highlight the region in the graph corresponding to each cause. Zoom on a specific time frame through brushing. Double click to unzoom.
The dominating causes are Lightening and Arson. It is interesting to note that one of them is natural while the other is triggered by humans.

How have the effects of the fires changed over the years at a national scale?

This visualization helps to explore how the wildfires have affected the federal spending, time to contain the fires and area incinerated at a national scale over the years. Hover to get the information about a year or Brush to select multiple bars within an area to see the trends.


The moving average line (average of 3 years) shows that the all the above mentioned quantities show an upward trend. The wildfires are affecting the national resources such as land and taxpayer's money at a greater extent each year. More time and effort is also required each year to contain these wildfires which lead to heavy losses.

How do different states compare?

Use this tool to explore how different states measure up. Select a data attribute to view by and click different tiles to bring up detailed metrics for each state.

All states are drawn on the same scale unless otherwise noted with a "\\" on the y-axis.

Different regions are disproportionately affected by wildfires. Western states tend to lose more land to fire on a yearly basis and spend more time fighting fires than their counterparts on the East Coast. Southern states, on the other hand, have to contend with smaller yet more numerous fires. While Midwest states seem relatively fire-free in comparison, many of those states, such as Michigan, are still losing upwards of 1,000 acres of land to fire every year.

What's more, states like Texas and Washington are observing an increase in the amount of land burned away by wildfires every year. Other states, like Idaho and Oregon, are spending more time in recent years to contain fires.
